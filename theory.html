Data Encryption Standard (DES): Extended Theory

What is DES?

The Data Encryption Standard (DES) is one of the most historically significant symmetric-key algorithms in the field of cryptography. Introduced in the 1970s, it set a global precedent for secure electronic communication. It functions as a symmetric block cipher, which means the same key is used for both encryption and decryption, and data is processed in fixed-size blocks—in this case, 64 bits at a time.

Who Invented DES and When?

DES was developed by IBM in the early 1970s, based on their internal project named Lucifer. The U.S. National Bureau of Standards (now NIST) adopted it as a federal standard in 1977 for non-classified governmental and commercial use. The algorithm was reviewed and modified with input from the NSA before becoming a standard.

Why Was DES Created?

During the 1970s, with the rise of computing in business and government, there was a growing need to protect digital information. Before DES, there was no widely accepted method for encrypting data electronically. DES provided a standardized and vetted method for ensuring confidentiality, which was especially crucial in banking, telecommunications, and government communication.

Basic Parameters of DES

  • Block size: 64 bits (plaintext and ciphertext)
  • Key size: 64 bits, but only 56 bits are used for actual encryption; the remaining 8 bits are parity checks.
  • Number of rounds: 16
  • Structure: Feistel network
  • Type: Symmetric block cipher

How Does DES Work? Step-by-Step Process

  1. Initial Permutation (IP): Rearranges the bits of the 64-bit plaintext block using a predefined table to enhance diffusion.
  2. Splitting: The block is divided into two halves: Left (L) and Right (R), each 32 bits.
  3. Key Schedule: The 56-bit key is processed to generate 16 unique 48-bit subkeys for the 16 rounds.
  4. Feistel Rounds (16 rounds): Each round consists of:
    • Expanding R from 32 to 48 bits (E-expansion)
    • XORing expanded R with the round key
    • Passing the result through 8 S-boxes (each mapping 6 bits to 4 bits)
    • Permuting the result using a P-box
    • XORing the output with L, and swapping halves
  5. Final Permutation (IP⁻¹): The final output of the 16th round is permuted again to produce the ciphertext.

The Key Schedule in Detail

The original 64-bit key undergoes PC-1 permutation to remove parity bits, resulting in 56 bits. These bits are split into two 28-bit halves, each of which is shifted according to a schedule and permuted again with PC-2 to generate 48-bit subkeys used in each of the 16 rounds.

The S-Boxes and Confusion

One of the key cryptographic strengths of DES lies in its S-boxes, which introduce non-linearity. Each 6-bit input to an S-box is mapped to a 4-bit output. This substitution is irreversible, and it ensures that small changes in the input produce significant changes in the output, a principle known as the avalanche effect.

ASCII and Binary in DES

Since DES operates on binary data, textual input must first be converted using the ASCII table. Each ASCII character is represented by a unique 7- or 8-bit binary code.

Security Concerns and Legacy

By the late 1990s, DES had become vulnerable to brute-force attacks due to its 56-bit key. In 1998, the Electronic Frontier Foundation built a machine that cracked DES in under 24 hours. As a result, DES was deprecated for many uses and replaced by more secure alternatives like Triple DES (3DES) and eventually AES.
